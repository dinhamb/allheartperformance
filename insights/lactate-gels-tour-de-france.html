Why Are Tour de France Riders Suddenly Eating Lactate Gels?

Physiology  ·  July 12, 2026

By Braden Dinham — performance specialist, All Heart Performance

TL;DR

  • Lactate is not a waste product. It's a usable fuel for muscle, heart and brain, and a signalling molecule.
  • Tour de France riders are trialling exogenous lactate gels (around 40 g carbohydrate + 5 g lactate each), because lactate is absorbed via different transporters than glucose and fructose — potentially adding fuel without competing for gut absorption.
  • Elite riders appear to tolerate up to 20 g of lactate per hour without GI distress, which is a real formulation breakthrough.
  • A performance benefit is not proven. The published trials to date show little to no effect beyond a mild reduction in perceived effort.
  • For most athletes, the bigger gains remain carbohydrate intake, hydration, caffeine use and — above all — repeated practice of the plan.

Lactate was once blamed for burning legs, fading performance and next-day soreness.

Now some of the best cyclists in the world are deliberately swallowing it. That sounds backwards, but it isn't. It just reflects how far exercise physiology has moved from what most of us were taught. Lactate is not a toxic waste product, it's a fuel. It can be produced in one tissue, transported through the bloodstream and burned somewhere else entirely — skeletal muscle, the heart, the brain.

This raises a question that professional cycling is now testing in public:

If the body is already very good at using lactate as fuel, is there anything to gain by eating more of it?

Lactate Was Never Just Metabolic Waste

Much of the shift in our thinking came through the work of Dr George Brooks and the Lactate Shuttle Hypothesis.

The idea is straightforward. Lactate is constantly being produced, moved and reused around the body. One tissue makes it, another absorbs it, a third burns it. The heart uses it. Skeletal muscle uses it. Under the right conditions, so does the brain. It also acts as a signalling molecule, not just a substrate.

Endurance training improves how well you produce, transport and use it. The genuinely new question is what happens when you stop relying on the lactate you make and start swallowing it instead.

Why Bother With Exogenous Lactate At All?

Modern endurance fuelling already pushes carbohydrate intake hard, it's basically already a competitive fuelling game. Athletes combine glucose and fructose because they're absorbed through different intestinal transporters, which allows more total carbohydrate to be taken on than glucose alone would permit. At the elite end, riders are now consuming huge amounts per hour.

The constraint here is not how much energy a rider can carry, it's how much the gut can actually absorb. This is why lactate is now being looked at. It uses monocarboxylate (MCT) transporters — a different route entirely to the glucose/fructose transporters mentioned earlier. In theory, that means you could add usable fuel without competing for the same absorption pathway you've already maxed out.

The idea isn't to replace carbohydrate, it's to sit alongside it. That's an elegant piece of reasoning, the kind of solution that's beautiful in its simplicity. It is also (at this stage) still theory.

What's Actually In the Gels?

The barrier wasn't necessarily whether the body could use ingested lactate, it was whether anyone could stomach it.

Lactate is awkward to formulate. Taste, acidity, mineral load and GI tolerance have been huge barriers in every previous attempt — an early polylactate product in the 2000s was essentially undrinkable and delivered too little lactate to matter.

The current product driving the noise is ExoLactate, developed by Spanish sport scientist Aitor Viribay (formerly of INEOS Grenadiers) with food scientists from the Basque Culinary Center. Each gel reportedly contains around 40 g of carbohydrate and 5 g of lactate. Protocols of up to four gels an hour have been reported in elite use — 160 g of carbohydrate and 20 g of lactate per hour, apparently without GI distress.

If that tolerance holds up, it's a real formulation achievement. However, tolerating a product and benefiting from it are two different claims.

Does It Actually Make You Faster?

Here is the part the coverage tends to skip.

The published evidence on ingested lactate is thin and unimpressive. A 2024 randomised, double-blind crossover trial using an oral calcium-and-magnesium lactate supplement found no change in VO2peak, lactate threshold or ventilatory threshold, with only a modest increase in 20-minute time-trial work rate. A separate 2024 trial in trained cyclists using calcium lactate found no time-trial benefit at all — despite measurably raising blood bicarbonate and lowering perceived effort.

Read that last one carefully. The most consistent finding so far is that exogenous lactate behaves a bit like a mild buffer: it can make an effort feel easier without making you faster.

The manufacturer's own early data is more encouraging but should be read cautiously — small samples, unpublished, and averages driven by a couple of large individual responders. The proposed benefits — glycogen sparing, fuel for the heart and brain, better late-stage durability, improved recovery between stages — are all physiologically plausible. Every one of them is worth investigating. None of them is yet established in athletes who are already fuelling well.

Field practice regularly moves ahead of published literature, and it may well be vindicated here. But right now the hype is a long way ahead of the evidence, and I'd rather tell you that than sell you a story.

So Who Is This Actually For?

If a real benefit exists, I believe it belongs at the very top.

A Tour rider has an enormous energy demand, a trained gut, a rehearsed fuelling plan and 21 days of racing in which a one-percent effect compounds. When you have already optimised everything else, the margins are all that's left. That's the correct context for this product.

Most athletes are not in that context.

If you're inconsistently taking in 50–80 g of carbohydrate an hour, you do not have a lactate problem. You have a fuelling problem. Adding an exotic new substrate to a plan you haven't mastered is like fine-tuning the suspension on a car with a flat tyre. Harsh, I know, but fair.

And here's the part that gets lost when elite strategies go public: professional riders aren't just using different products. They get vastly more opportunities to practise. Long rides, camps, stage races, race simulations — repetition after repetition to train the gut and refine the plan. Most amateurs are lucky to get one proper race-fuelling rehearsal a fortnight.

The gap usually isn't knowledge, it's repetitive experience.

What Should You Actually Do?

Master the boring things first.

The Australian Institute of Sport classifies supplements by strength of evidence. Its Group A — the well-supported tier — includes carbohydrate sports foods, caffeine, creatine, beta-alanine, dietary nitrate and sodium bicarbonate for specific situations. Lactate gels are nowhere near that list, and won't be for some time.

Even within Group A, more is not automatically better. So before you go hunting for a lactate gel:

  • Work out how much carbohydrate you can actually tolerate per hour, and build towards it.
  • Practise your fuelling plan at realistic race intensity — not on easy rides.
  • Test your hydration strategy in the conditions you'll actually race in. Especially if you're racing a hot summer event.
  • Learn whether caffeine helps you, rather than adding it because everyone else does.
  • Then repeat all of it until it's automatic under pressure.

That's unglamorous. It's also where almost all of your available performance is sitting.

The Main Point

Lactate may well turn out to be a genuine addition to elite endurance fuelling. The physiology is sound, the absorption logic is clever, and the interest from professional cycling is real.

But the performance benefit of swallowing extra lactate is not settled, and the small amount of published evidence we have is relatively lukewarm.

For a WorldTour rider chasing the last available margin, it's worth exploring. For the rest of us, the bigger opportunity hasn't changed in twenty years: fuel consistently, practise under pressure, learn your gut, and get the proven strategies right before chasing the newest one.
