The Human Didn't Evolve. The System Did: How the Sub-2 Hour Marathon Was Finally Broken

Performance  ·  July 22, 2026

By Braden Dinham — performance specialist, All Heart Performance

It has been a few months since Sabastian Sawe broke the two-hour marathon barrier, and I have finally had the space to sit down and think about what it meant.

A fair bit has happened for me in the meantime. I was peaking for an event in April, had surgery soon after, and officially started two businesses at once, alongside the usual parenting and hospital work. Life moved on quickly, as it tends to do.

But I remember the moment clearly, I saw the headline and stopped for a moment. As a human achievement, it felt a little like landing on the moon. I reflected with a beer that night (I won't confess to the exact one I had in my fridge), but I will confess to feeling the significance of that historical achievement.

The reason it landed so strongly goes back about fifteen years, to when I was studying exercise science at university and one of the questions we wrestled with was how the two-hour marathon would eventually be broken.

Would it require a new kind of human? Perhaps some seven-foot specimen with an extraordinary stride length, but somehow the same running economy as the light, compact East African runners dominating the marathon. Would training science unlock another level of physiology? Would someone simply be born with a combination of VO₂ max, lactate threshold and running economy that had never existed before? Or would technology eventually change the equation?

At the time, we did not know. Now we have an answer.

The human did not suddenly evolve. The system around the human did.

First, the Human Still Matters

Before giving shoes, sports nutrition and technology too much credit, it is worth stating the obvious: Sabastian Sawe is an extraordinary athlete.

On 26 April 2026, he ran 1:59:30 at the London Marathon, becoming the first person to complete an official marathon in under two hours. Yomif Kejelcha was only eleven seconds behind him in 1:59:41 — remarkably, in his first ever marathon, the fastest debut in history — while Jacob Kiplimo ran 2:00:28, also inside the previous world record. The fact that three athletes beat the old mark on the same day, one of them a debutant, tells us something important. This was bigger than one exceptional performance. The conditions for elite marathon running had clearly moved forward, but Sawe was still the best athlete in an extraordinary field.

There is always a danger when discussing technology that we make the athlete sound interchangeable, as though the right combination of carbon plate, carbohydrate gel and sports scientist could be strapped onto anyone with sufficient aerobic capacity.

The shoes did not run 42.2 kilometres (well, not by themselves!). The gels and sports scientists did not run it, Sawe did. But he did so inside a performance environment that looks very different from the one we were studying fifteen years ago.

The Running Shoe Pendulum

The evolution of the running shoe is almost comical when viewed across the last two decades, because for a long time the industry seemed to lurch from one confident philosophy to another.

There was a period when shoe prescription was dominated by concepts such as foot type, arch height, pronation and the wet-foot test. Runners were often divided into neat categories and prescribed neutral, stability or motion-control shoes according to the shape of their foot or the degree to which they supposedly pronated.

Then the evidence began to make that tidy system look much less certain.

My favourite study involving military recruits tested whether assigning shoes according to arch height or plantar foot shape actually reduced injuries during basic training. It did not, and thus I binned my chunky pair of Brooks Adrenaline shoes. That did not prove that shoes were irrelevant, nor did it single-handedly launch the barefoot movement, but it helped undermine confidence in the idea that a footprint could reliably tell us exactly what sort of shoe a runner needed.

Around the same time, almost every possible philosophy of footwear seemed to be competing for attention. Some companies wanted to control movement with increasingly structured shoes. Others were experimenting with visible mechanical technologies that looked impressive but had far less evidence behind an actual improvement in speed.

There were even shoes such as MBTs, with heavily curved rocker soles designed around the idea of recreating the instability of walking on soft ground or sand. The theory was that making the ground beneath you less stable would force more muscular activity and perhaps restore something modern flat surfaces had taken away. While never becoming anything more than a niche shoe, I wonder whether the idea was grown from the same seed that brought us the super shoes in later years.

Then came the minimalist revolution.

The minimalist idea was seductive, and many of us subscribed wholeheartedly. Humans had evolved to run barefoot, modern shoes were interfering with natural mechanics, and perhaps the answer was simply less shoe. Barefoot running, Vibram FiveFingers, zero-drop designs and forefoot striking became part of mainstream running culture. I still have a pair of Newtons today from that era, and I remember comparing feet with some of the dedicated truly barefoot runners.

There was legitimate science underneath some of it, including Daniel Lieberman's 2010 journal article in Nature. The problem came when interesting observations about biomechanics and foot strike were stretched into much broader claims about injury prevention, ideal running form and the idea that less shoe was inherently more natural and therefore better.

Then the pendulum swung violently in the opposite direction. Instead of less shoe, suddenly the fastest runners in the world wanted more: more foam, more stack height, more energy return, and a carbon-fibre plate running through the middle of it. I was curious if shoes like Adidas Bounce would be more than a passing fad, or would their highlight be a fun little stick figure game on the website.

The real turning point was Nike's Breaking2 project in May 2017, when Eliud Kipchoge ran 2:00:25 around the Monza motor racing circuit wearing a prototype of what became the Nike Vaporfly 4%.

The significance was not simply that the shoe looked different or came with a clever marketing campaign. It produced a measurable change in running economy. Research on the Vaporfly showed roughly a 4% reduction in the energetic cost of running compared with other contemporary marathon racing shoes. Individual responses varied, and a 4% improvement in running economy does not translate directly into a 4% faster marathon, but this was no longer simply a shoe company telling us its latest foam looked springier. There was a measurable physiological effect, and that changed the conversation.

What Actually Makes a Super Shoe Work?

The early explanation was appealingly simple. Soft, highly responsive PEBA-based foam returned more energy, the carbon-fibre plate acted like a stiff lever, and putting the two together made the runner more efficient. My simple mind thought of it like a pogo stick: hard metal pegs to push on, springy coil underneath to bounce with.

The reality appears to be more complicated, which actually makes the story more interesting. No single element was the hero: foam, plate, lightweight construction, geometry and rocker shape all combining in a single coherent system. The plate contributes to the overall system, but it was the combined system that resulted in a measurable improvement in running economy.

Once Nike demonstrated that it worked, the rest of the industry had to respond. By 2020, the technology had become controversial enough that World Athletics introduced rules limiting road-racing shoes to a maximum sole thickness of 40 millimetres and a single embedded rigid plate. The question was no longer whether super shoes worked and whether athletes could tolerate them without injury (much to the surprise of my 2010 self), the question was how much technological assistance was too much.

From Monopoly to a New Normal

For a while, Nike had the advantage. Then everyone caught up.

Adidas, ASICS, Puma, Saucony, New Balance and others developed their own high-stack, lightweight, highly responsive racing shoes, and the super shoe stopped being a strange Nike experiment and became the expected footwear of elite road running.

That matters because Sawe's achievement did not come from simply wearing the one secret shoe nobody else could access. He ran in Adidas' Adizero Adios Pro Evo 3. The performance system had evolved beyond one company, and advanced footwear technology had become part of the sport itself.

Observational research suggests that the performance effect is not trivial. Estimates vary, but some studies have found marathon improvements in the range of roughly 1–3%, depending on the athlete and the comparison shoe. At elite marathon level, that can represent minutes rather than seconds.

But the shoes are still only part of the answer, because while the footwear industry was transforming, another revolution was quietly happening.

The Other Revolution Was Happening in the Gut

When I was studying exercise science fifteen years ago, endurance fuelling advice was considerably more conservative than much of what we now see at the elite end of endurance sport.

We're all aware that fuelling is currently evolving, and endurance events are almost becoming competitive eating events. But I still have the textbook from that time confidently labelling 90g as the maximum hourly absorption in humans.

That number made perfect sense in the context of what we understood then. Around 60 grams per hour was a familiar limit when glucose was used alone, while higher intakes became possible through multiple transportable carbohydrates, particularly combinations of glucose and fructose using different intestinal transport systems. Ninety grams per hour felt close to the ceiling for transporters and tolerance.

Now, the leading edge of endurance nutrition is pushing well beyond it. Research and elite practice have explored carbohydrate intakes around 120 grams per hour and sometimes considerably more, although the optimal amount still depends heavily on the athlete, the sport, the duration and intensity of the event, and the ability of the gut to tolerate such large quantities under stress.

This matters because a marathon runner trying to sustain two-hour pace is not simply trying to maximise VO₂ max. The athlete also has to preserve carbohydrate availability, manage gastrointestinal tolerance, maintain fluid balance, regulate heat and somehow keep running at better than 21 kilometres per hour while all of those systems are being stressed at once.

Better fuelling does not make an ordinary runner a two-hour marathoner, but at the extreme edge of human performance, delaying the point at which carbohydrate availability becomes limiting can be enormously important.

Perhaps just as important as the fuel itself is the opportunity to practise using it. Elite athletes repeatedly rehearse race fuelling during long sessions, training camps, race simulations and competition. They train the gut, test different carbohydrate concentrations, practise at race intensity and learn what they can tolerate when the body is already under substantial physiological stress. Most amateur athletes simply do not get the same number of repetitions.

That is not as visually interesting as a carbon plate, but it is part of the same story. The performance system got better.

Better Pacing, Better Data and Better Integration

The next part of the story is less dramatic because there is no single product to point at. There was no day when better pacing was suddenly invented, and nobody launched Recovery 4%.

What has changed is the integration of performance science.

Athletes and coaches now have better access to physiological testing, training-load data, altitude strategies, biomechanics, recovery monitoring, weather forecasting, course modelling and race analytics. None of those things alone breaks two hours, but together they reduce the number of avoidable mistakes and allow an athlete to arrive at the start line fitter, better fuelled, better paced, better recovered, wearing more efficient shoes and with a clearer understanding of exactly what the task requires.

That is often what high performance actually looks like. Not one spectacular breakthrough, but several small advantages being stacked together until something previously impossible becomes possible.

And Then There Is Sawe

This is where the story has to return to the athlete, because it would be lazy to explain a 1:59:30 marathon entirely through shoes and carbohydrate.

Plenty of runners wore super shoes before Sawe. Plenty had access to elite sports nutrition, world-class coaches, altitude camps and sophisticated performance data.

They did not run 1:59:30.

Sawe's performance was extraordinary even by the standards of extraordinary runners. He did not cling to a suicidal opening pace and stumble across the line after surviving the final kilometres. He passed halfway in 60:29, ran the second half in 59:01, and broke decisively with a little over a mile remaining. A negative split, in the fastest marathon ever run.

That suggests something beyond maximal aerobic capacity alone. It speaks to durability (another recent buzz word): the ability to preserve performance deep into an event while fatigue is accumulating and other athletes are beginning to lose theirs.

His achievement belongs to him. But the conditions that made an achievement like his possible belong to an entire era.

What This Means If You Are Not Running 1:59

It would be easy to file all of this away as elite trivia, interesting but irrelevant to anyone who is not competing as a sponsored, genetically gifted superhuman. I think that would be the wrong lesson.

The principle that broke the two-hour barrier scales down remarkably well: performance improves when the system around the athlete improves. Not through one magic purchase, but through several unglamorous advantages stacked together.

For an everyday runner or endurance athlete, the system looks like this: Shoes that suit you and your event, chosen on evidence rather than marketing philosophy. A fuelling strategy you have actually practised in training, not one you are trying for the first time on race morning. Training load that builds consistently instead of lurching between enthusiasm and injury. Recovery treated as part of the program rather than a sign of weakness. Pacing built on data and honesty rather than hope.

None of those things will take minutes off your marathon overnight. Together, over a season, they routinely do. That is the entire premise behind how I coach: the athlete supplies the effort, and the job of the system is to make sure none of that effort is wasted.

Sawe needed a better-engineered system to find those last sixty-five seconds. Most of us have way more than sixty-five seconds sitting in our margin for improvement.

So What Actually Broke the Two-Hour Barrier?

It's a cliche, but I don't think it was a single thing. It obviously requires a genetic outlier like Sawe, but I'm not sure it happens without the knowledge and performance advancements built over previous decades. It certainly wasn't that humans suddenly evolved in the fifteen years between the time I sat in a university classroom asking the question and April 2026.

It was the interaction of an extraordinary athlete with an increasingly sophisticated performance system. And after all of that, an exceptional athlete still had to execute it.

That, for me, is the answer to the question we were asking fifteen years ago. We did not need to wait for a seven-foot running machine to be born, and we did not need human evolution to produce a new species of marathoner. We needed an extraordinary human operating inside a better-engineered system. In April three runners showed us what that could look like, and I still think that was worth raising a beer to.
